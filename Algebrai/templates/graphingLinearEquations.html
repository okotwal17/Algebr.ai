What is a Linear Equation?

A linear equation is an algebraic equation in which the highest exponent of the variable is one. When graphed, these equations produce a straight line.

Slope-Intercept Form

The most common form of a linear equation is called the slope-intercept form, written as:

y = mx + b

Where:

  • m is the slope (the steepness of the line).
  • b is the y-intercept (the point where the line crosses the y-axis).

Understanding Slope

The slope of a line measures its steepness. It is the ratio of the vertical change to the horizontal change between two points on the line. The formula for slope is:

slope (m) = (y₂ - y₁) / (x₂ - x₁)

Example: If the slope is 2, for every 1 unit you move to the right, the line moves up 2 units.

Plotting a Linear Equation

To graph a linear equation, follow these steps:

  1. Find the y-intercept (b) and plot the point where the line crosses the y-axis.
  2. Use the slope (m) to find another point. For example, if the slope is 2, from the y-intercept, move up 2 units and 1 unit to the right to find the next point.
  3. Draw a straight line through the points.

Graphing Example

Let's graph the equation:

y = 2x + 3

In this equation:

  • The slope m is 2 (which means "rise 2, run 1").
  • The y-intercept b is 3 (so the line crosses the y-axis at y = 3).

Start by plotting the point (0, 3). Then, from this point, move up 2 units and 1 unit to the right to find the next point (1, 5). Draw the line through these points.
